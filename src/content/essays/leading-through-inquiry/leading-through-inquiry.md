If you read enough leadership advice, you start to notice a pattern.

Leaders are told to either ask more questions or give clearer direction. One promises ownership and growth. The other promises speed and alignment.

Both are useful. Both are incomplete.

The real work sits in between. It isn't about picking a style. It's about recognizing what the moment actually needs, and adjusting.

Nowhere is that harder than in one-on-ones.

## The One-on-One That Taught Me This

A few years ago, someone on my team missed a deadline for the second time in a month.

The first time, I'd asked questions. What happened, what got in the way, what would they do differently. It felt like the right call, and I still think it was. But going into the second conversation, I caught myself preparing more questions. Same format, same tone, same "let's explore this together" energy.

Halfway through writing my notes, I stopped.

I already knew what needed to happen. Nothing left to explore. What I was about to do was ask questions I didn't need answers to, just to avoid saying the thing directly.

That one-on-one is where this essay actually started.

## Why Questions Fail In One-on-Ones

One-on-ones are overloaded conversations. They carry performance, trust, power, safety, and expectation all at once. A question that sounds neutral in theory can land very differently in practice.

This is where many new leads struggle. They keep asking open questions when clarity is needed. Or they give direction too early, before understanding how the other person is actually thinking.

The issue was never *whether* to ask questions. It's what problem the question is meant to solve.

In every one-on-one, a question has one of two jobs.

The first is to help **you** understand reality. You need context, facts, the other person's read on the situation.

The second is to help **them** think. Good questions slow people down. They challenge assumptions. They expose blind spots. Sometimes they just give someone enough room to hear themselves think out loud.

> The mistake is expecting the same question to do both jobs equally well.

## There Are Only Two Situations That Matter

In practice, leadership conversations fall into two categories.

Either you don't yet understand the problem well enough. Or you understand it well enough, and now something needs to change.

Questions serve the first situation. Direction serves the second.

Most leadership mistakes come from mixing the two.

## Scenario One: The First Time It Happens

Imagine someone on your team misses an expectation.

Maybe they underestimated a task. Maybe communication broke down somewhere. Maybe the work came in below what you expected.

It's the first time. The impact is limited. And, if you're honest, you already have an idea of how you'd solve it.

Resist the urge. Your first job isn't to fix the problem. It's to understand how they're thinking about it. Instead of saying:

> "Next time, you should do X."

Try asking:

- How are you thinking about what happened?
- What do you think contributed to it?
- Looking back, what would you do differently?
- What options have you considered?
- If you had to solve this tomorrow, what would you try first?

None of these are fishing for your preferred answer. They're helping you see the other person's mental model, which matters more than whether they land on the same solution you had in mind.

A surprising number of people already know what went wrong. What they've rarely been asked is *how* they got there.

Sometimes the solution they propose is different from yours. If the downside is acceptable, let them try it. Not because every solution is equally good, but because ownership often teaches more than correctness does.

A leader's goal isn't to produce perfect decisions every time. It's to develop people who can make good ones without you — the same shift I write about in [[time-off-for-leaders|what your team's behavior looks like when you're not around]].

## Knowing When To Stop Asking

This is the part leadership books rarely cover.

Exploration shouldn't continue forever. Eventually, the questions stop producing new understanding. A few signals tell you that moment has arrived:

- You understand how the person is reasoning, even if you disagree with it.
- The problem is no longer ambiguous.
- The cost of continuing to experiment has gotten too high.

That's the moment to change gears. Not because coaching failed. Because coaching did its job.

## Scenario Two: When The Stakes Change

Now imagine the same issue happens again. Or the consequences are simply bigger this time.

A customer was affected. The rest of the team had to compensate. Trust has started to erode.

You still begin with questions. But they're different questions. Instead of exploring possibilities, you're confirming reality.

- Is there context I'm missing?
- Is there anything that changes how we should view this?
- Do you see the situation differently than I do?

These exist to close blind spots, not open them. Once they've been answered, you stop asking.

This is where leadership gets uncomfortable. You move from coaching into authority. You start saying things like:

> "Here's what needs to change."
>
> "This is the standard I expect going forward."
>
> "This is how we're going to handle situations like this."
>
> "If this doesn't improve, here's what happens next."

Notice these aren't questions anymore. They're not trying to generate insight. They're creating clarity.

Many leads hesitate here. They worry that giving direction means abandoning coaching.

It doesn't. It means the purpose of the conversation changed.

## The Biggest Mistake Leaders Make

The biggest mistake isn't giving too much direction. It isn't asking too many questions either.

It's asking coaching questions after the leadership decision has already been made.

Sometimes you already know what needs to happen. Expectations need resetting. A performance issue needs addressing. Accountability is required. And yet you keep asking open questions, because it feels kinder.

In reality, it often creates more uncertainty, not less. The employee leaves wondering if there's actually a problem. Or worse, believing they own a decision that was never really theirs to make.

Curiosity becomes a way to delay responsibility. That isn't coaching. That's avoidance, and it's a habit I try to catch in myself the way I'd catch it when [[mastering-effective-feedback-facts-feelings-curiosity|giving feedback without clear intent]].

## Questions Are Not Better Than Direction

There's a temptation to treat questions as the more enlightened form of leadership.

They're not. Questions are simply tools. Sometimes they encourage thinking. Sometimes they waste time.

Direction is also a tool. Sometimes it shuts people down. Sometimes it gives them exactly the certainty they need.

Neither is inherently better. What matters is knowing which one the moment requires — and that judgment call is a lot of what I mean when I talk about [[gaming-made-me-better-leader|leadership as facilitation rather than command]].

## A Simple Check Before Every Question

Before asking another question, pause for a second and ask yourself:

Am I trying to understand? Am I trying to help them think? Or am I avoiding a decision I already know I need to make?

The answer usually tells you whether your next sentence should end with a question mark or a full stop.
